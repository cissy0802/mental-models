OODA Loop

Observe · Orient · Decide · Act — John Boyd's tempo weapon
Detail

OODA is not just a "decision framework." Boyd engineered "completing a loop faster than your opponent" into a strategic weapon. When your loop is shorter than the adversary's, every one of their decisions targets a reality that has already passed. Their actions become persistently irrelevant; they crack psychologically first. Physical contest turns into a tempo contest.

Non-trivial insights: ① The bottleneck is almost always Orient, not Decide. Raw signals from Observe must pass through your mental models / culture / experience before they become actionable understanding. Most people train themselves to "decide faster" — wrong leverage point. Whether your model is accurate and how often it updates determines everything downstream of Decide. ② "Faster" is not more effort — it's a more frequently updated model. Same signal: a person with a stale model interprets it as A; a person with a fresh model interprets it as B and is already in position by the time the first acts. This is structurally identical to stale state in distributed systems — you act on the world as it was 100ms ago. ③ Counterintuitive: sometimes deliberately slowing down wins. You let the opponent commit on a wrong model, then exploit their misalignment. Boyd called this "operating inside the opponent's OODA loop." ④ The AI-era leverage: put the LLM into the Orient stage, not the Act stage. Let it help you reconstruct understanding (counterexamples, blind spots, second-order consequences) — an order of magnitude more leverage than having it "decide" or "type for you."

Practice: ① After every failed decision, diagnose by stage: "Did Observe miss a signal? Was the Orient model stale? Did Decide hesitate? Was Act slow?" 80% of the time the answer is Orient — your world model didn't keep up. ② In fast-changing domains (AI, markets, a child's current stage), schedule a monthly "active re-Orient" — drop the existing labels, just observe for a week before judging again. ③ To shorten the loop: shorten Orient first (upgrade model). Don't first force Decide to be faster.

Orient is the only stage you can deliberately train — and the most-overlooked bottleneck
Classic example

Korean War, F-86 vs MiG-15. The MiG-15 was superior in climb rate, turn rate, and firepower; pilot training was comparable. Yet the combat kill ratio was roughly 10:1 in favor of the F-86. Boyd's analysis: the F-86's full bubble canopy (faster Observe) and hydraulic controls (a few hundred ms faster maneuver switching — faster Act) shortened each loop by a small amount. Compounded over rounds, the MiG pilot found every maneuver responding to the F-86's previous move. He was squeezed out of his decision space and forced into reflex actions, then locked and killed. Not stronger at any single point — shorter loop.

Scenario · BigCat

① AI-era product competition: two teams, same resources — the winner is whoever has the shorter "ship → observe user signal → re-orient understanding → ship again" loop. AI has flattened Decide and Act; the only remaining bottleneck is Orient. So "ideating / counter-arguing / stress-testing assumptions with AI" has much higher leverage than "having AI write more code." ② Parenting: your reaction to a child's "problem behavior" often comes from a model you oriented to six months ago — "this is the kind of child she is." She has changed, your model hasn't. Every intervention now hits a stale target; the harder you push, the worse the misalignment. A monthly "re-orient" (drop labels, just observe for a week) beats ten parenting books. ③ Distributed systems: service-mesh health check frequency = the system's OODA. Shorter interval → faster failure detection, earlier traffic shift; longer interval → routing decisions based on a stale topology — exact same bug as "stale model." ④ Investing: same earnings report — whoever updates their model first acts the next day; whoever is still on last year's narrative is late, and by the time they move, the price has run.

Reflection

The biggest cognitive misalignment you had in the last three months — which stage of OODA caused it? Observe (missed signal)? Orient (stale model)? Decide (hesitation)? Act (slow execution)? Most people find their answers cluster at one stage — that's where the training should go.

Center of Gravity (Clausewitz)

Schwerpunkt — the pivot the entire system stands on
Detail

Clausewitz, On War: every adversarial system has a "center of gravity" — force converges there, morale flows from there, everything else organizes around it. Remove the center, the whole thing collapses. Hit the wrong node and 10× the effort just turns into attrition. CoG is the specialized form of "leverage point" in adversarial contexts.

Non-trivial insights: ① CoG is not "the largest thing" — it's "the node the whole system stands on." These often don't coincide. The visible giant is often the surface; what holds it up may be an unassuming back-line node (a rail junction, a key person, a trust link). ② Each system usually has only 1–2 real centers of gravity. The work of every great strategist can be compressed into one repeated question: "What is this system's center of gravity?" Most failed strategy isn't a wrong step — it's pushing on the wrong point from the start. ③ CoG can be non-physical — morale, an alliance, a leader's prestige, a supply line, an information channel. Germany's WWII CoG wasn't combat strength; it was industrial output and the railway network. ④ Counterintuitive: you also have only 1–2 centers of gravity. Most people spread effort across 10 things and don't know which one collapsing would take down the whole. That's spreading defense evenly across every node — the critical ones are necessarily under-defended. Identifying your own CoG is the start of defensive strategy. ⑤ The loud thing is rarely the CoG. Real centers of gravity tend to be silent, taken-for-granted, "just there" — which is why they get ignored, and why they are the leverage point.

Practice: ① Facing complexity, run a CoG quiz: "If I could remove only one node to destabilize this system, which?" Then: "Is 80% of my resource on that, or on secondary nodes?" ② Self-audit: what is the CoG of your work / life? (1–2 key capabilities? 1–2 key clients? Sleep? Closest relationship?) These are what cannot be allowed to fail — the rest can absorb shock. ③ Distrust "loudness-driven priority." Real CoGs don't shout. Run a "silent CoG audit" quarterly.

Find the CoG = find the node whose removal makes the system unable to stand
Classic example

D-Day, 1944. The Allies did not attack the German army's strongest point — they landed at lightly defended Normandy. But Normandy itself wasn't the CoG; it was the shortest path to Germany's real CoG (the Ruhr industrial region). This is the modern operationalization of Clausewitz: locate the CoG, design the minimum-cost path to it, concentrate resources on that path. Counter-example: WWI's Western Front — both sides smeared resources evenly across a 700 km line for "general advance." No CoG, no path, four years of stalemate and millions of casualties.

Scenario · BigCat

① Distributed systems: every production system has a "CoG service" — if it dies, the whole site dies (gateway? auth? metadata store? config center?). Identify it, give it the thickest redundancy, the strictest SLO, the most frequent drills. Other services can tolerate looser standards. Resources aren't spread evenly — they're allocated by CoG. SRE mis-allocation almost always stems from never identifying the CoG. ② AI super-individual: your CoG is almost certainly not technical depth — AI gives you free leverage on depth. Your CoG is "judgment + relationship network + attention quality" — the three things AI can't replace. Most deliberate practice belongs here. ③ Family: a child's developmental CoG is usually quality of the parent-child relationship + autonomy, not the number of classes or hours of study. CoG stable, everything else can absorb experimentation; CoG broken, no skill stack saves you. Many parents pour resources into surface nodes. ④ Personal health: for many adults, the CoG is sleep — once it breaks, exercise / diet / mood all chain-collapse. But training time goes to diet and exercise because they're "visible." Sleep is silent — so ignored — but it's the CoG.

Reflection

The thing making you most anxious right now — where is its CoG? Of your time / attention / money in the last week, what fraction actually went to the CoG vs. to surface nodes? If the gap is large, what is the first reallocation you can make next week?

Fog of War

Complete information is structurally impossible, not temporarily scarce
Detail

Clausewitz: "Three quarters of the things on which action in war is based lie in a fog of uncertainty." This isn't "we didn't gather enough intel this time" — it's the structural unattainability of complete information in adversarial systems. Waiting until everything is clear = never deciding. Fog is a structural fact, not a removable defect.

Non-trivial insights: ① Distinguish two kinds of uncertainty: (a) removable — more research / one more test will resolve it; (b) structural — no amount of waiting resolves it. Most stuck decisions are treating (b) as if it were (a): expecting that waiting will clear it up, but only burning energy and giving the opponent time. ② "Let me research more" is often a disguise for avoiding the decision. Ask: "If I wait one more week, how much actionable new signal will I actually get?" The honest answer is usually close to zero. ③ Fog isn't "low information" — it's low SNR. AI era: 100× more signal, 1000× more noise. Fog got thicker, not thinner. More gathering doesn't help; better signal discrimination does. ④ Fog is bidirectional: your opponent has fog too. "Thicken their fog" has the same leverage as "thin your own" — that's the fundamental role of deception / misdirection in war. ⑤ Fog dictates the right strategy shape: thick fog → antifragile (diverse, reversible, low-cost trials), not optimal (a single-point bet that would be best given complete information). Most failures are running a "complete-information strategy" inside fog. ⑥ The illusion of complete information is more lethal than missing information. Believing you understand your opponent / the market / your child, then placing a large bet on that false clarity.

Practice: ① Before deciding, ask twice: "What kind of fog is this? Will more research actually remove it?" If structural, switch to an antifragile frame (small experiments, preserved reversibility). ② Set a decision SLA: complex decisions get N days of research max — then commit on current posterior. Fog stops being an excuse for indefinite delay. ③ Be suspicious of moments when "I suddenly feel I get it." Often your brain just wrote a narrative inside the fog — the fog didn't lift.

Classic example

Napoleon's 1812 Russia campaign. Intelligence said the Russians would stand and fight — instead they burned their own towns and retreated, dragging the French army to Moscow and then torching it, leaving Napoleon an empty city. Half the intel was wrong. But Napoleon's deeper error wasn't missing information — it was believing he understood Russian logic. The illusion of complete information let him drop antifragile measures (enough supplies, a planned retreat line) and run "the optimal advance to Moscow." Of the ~600,000 men who marched in, ~500,000 died — most during the retreat. The cost of running a complete-information strategy inside fog.

Scenario · BigCat

① Startup / product: you never know what users "really want" — this is structural fog. "One more round of user research" usually doesn't remove it, just adds sunk cost and delay. MVP thinking is fog's engineered antidote — exchange the fastest, cheapest move for the most truthful signal (real clicks / payments beat ten interviews). ② Investing: macro is always foggy. "Waiting until things are clear" is functionally equivalent to missing the move — by the time clarity arrives, the opportunity is gone. Margin of safety + diversification is operationalized antifragility, not timidity. ③ AI collaboration: LLM answers are always in fog — they may hallucinate. Accept this as structural (not "better prompts will solve it"), and design verification loops (have AI give two opposing answers, have a second AI critique, let code/tests be the final judge) rather than chasing the "perfect prompt." ④ Parenting: you'll never know the real 10-year impact of any intervention (this class, screen-time limits, a school change). The fog won't lift. What you can do: pick antifragile directions (diverse exploration > single bets), preserve reversibility, let her build her own posterior across multiple cycles. Every "perfect parenting plan" is a fog illusion. ⑤ Distributed systems: monitoring reports the system state from N seconds ago, sampled, with noise. Every SRE decision is made inside fog — good on-call engineers know this; bad ones think they're seeing ground truth.

Reflection

A decision you've recently been deferring "to be more sure" — unpack it: is the fog (a) removable or (b) structural? If (b), have you set a decision SLA? If not, set one now: by time X, you commit on current posterior, regardless.

Indirect Approach

Liddell Hart — decisive victory comes from making the opponent lose balance, not from collision
Detail

Liddell Hart systematically studied 280+ decisive battles from antiquity through WWII. His conclusion was almost monotonic: direct approaches (hitting the opponent's strongest point) almost never produced decisive victory. All decisive victories first put the opponent off-balance — psychologically and physically — and only then applied force. "Indirect" isn't avoiding the problem; it's finding a line of advance along which the opponent is continuously reactive.

Non-trivial insights: ① The direct approach = your strength colliding with their strength. The result is almost certainly attrition. WWI's Western Front was the industrialized version of the direct approach: 15 million dead for a line that didn't move. Most "brave assaults" are strategically wasted. ② Essence of indirect: induce dislocation first, then strike. Dislocation can be physical (showing up where they aren't), psychological (making them defend against the wrong threat), or temporal (forcing them to commit the wrong resources first). Once dislocated, the leverage of your force multiplies. ③ "Courage" is not a synonym for "direct." The real masters save courage for "betting at impossible angles." When indirect is available, take it; commit to direct only after structural advantage is already accumulated. ④ Structural leverage for small players: a small player can't win on a large player's main battlefield, but the large player can't be 100% present on every secondary battlefield. The indirect approach is the small player's only engineered path. Walmart, Amazon, TikTok all started indirect. ⑤ Counterintuitive: the "weakest point you see" often isn't the right direction for indirect approach. The right one is "weakest point that is also the channel to their CoG" (combining with Card 2). A standalone weak point with no strategic value is a trap.

Practice: ① Every time the urge to "hit it head-on" arises, spend 30 seconds asking: "Is there a path that would force the opponent into a reactive posture next? " Not finding one means 80% of the time you haven't looked hard enough — not that it doesn't exist. ② Designing any key project, avoid the strongest competitor's main battlefield. Find a secondary field they don't fully occupy, build structural advantage there (not skill advantage — structural, so they can't quickly relocate), then migrate. ③ Parenting: when you want to use "lecturing + pressure + reward" to force a behavior change — that's direct, and usually low-yield. Change the environment, change her peers, model it yourself, give her choice — most parenting "techniques" are just operationalized indirect approach.

Indirect ≠ just "going around" — it is "going around + ending on the CoG"
Classic example

Hannibal crossing the Alps. Rome expected him to come by sea or through the plains of northern Italy — both heavily prepared, strongest-defense directions. Hannibal chose to cross the Alps in winter, at the cost of a third of his army, and emerged in Rome's softest interior. The pre-Cannae victories weren't about tactical detail — they were about his position making all of Rome's preparation instantly irrelevant. Rome ran to catch him, reshuffled defenses, became reactive — every contact afterward happened under a structural disadvantage. The cleanest case of the indirect approach: one extreme detour buys N subsequent engagements fought from structural advantage.

Scenario · BigCat

① AI-era personal path: don't go head-to-head with big labs on "whose LLM is better" — find their blind spots (vertical domains, long tail, complex workflows, places where strong domain judgment dominates). Someone with "AI + a specific vertical + their own judgment" is nearly unbeatable head-on — unless you also build your own triangle, on a secondary battlefield they aren't present on, accumulate structural advantage, then migrate. ② Distributed systems: Spanner head-on on "global strong consistency + high availability" is nearly hopeless. TiDB, CockroachDB, Aurora all run indirect — "eventual consistency is enough," "geographically local strong consistency," "optimize for specific query patterns" — building in secondary scenarios Google won't directly contest, then migrating up. ③ Investing: don't compete head-on with top institutions on large caps or hot sectors for info and speed — find the spaces they can't or won't enter (illiquid, small, non-standard, legal but regulatorily inconvenient). Buffett, Klarman, and Marks all started indirect. ④ Parenting: head-on insistence on "you must do X" usually loses twice — the goal and the relationship. Indirect = change the environment, change the peer composition, model it yourself, give her real choices, change the activation context. Most "parenting tips" are just operationalized indirect approach. ⑤ Team management: trying to push a change-averse colleague directly typically triggers resistance. Indirect = change the behavior of the people around them, give them a low-cost entry path, let them generate the "I want to change" feeling themselves.

Reflection

Something you've been brute-forcing recently — if direct has been demonstrably low-yield, what would the indirect look like? What kind of detour would put the other side (or the system, or the child, or yourself) in a reactive position by the time you push? And is your detour actually heading toward the CoG, or is it just an unrelated bypass?
