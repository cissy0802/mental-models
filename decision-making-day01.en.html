Mental Models Daily: Decision-Making

2026-05-25 · Day 01

First Principles Thinking

Demoting "inherited assumptions" back into suspect variables
Deep Dive

Aristotle's plain definition has been underrated by being plain: "the first basis from which a thing is known" — the irreducible starting point of knowledge. The hard part isn't deduction from axioms; it's recognizing which of your "axioms" are actually inherited assumptions absorbed from the surrounding consensus. Most "principled derivations" are really restatements of the current school's premises — you think you're deducing; you're transcribing.

Non-trivial insights: ① The real first-principles move is demotion, not deduction. Re-tagging a "fact" as a "suspect variable" is the scarce cognitive act. Musk's battery-cost example isn't impressive for the physics (high-school chemistry suffices) — it's impressive because he refused to accept "$600/kWh" as input when every competitor took it as given. ② Compiler analogy: most judgments do peephole optimization at the IR layer; first principles thinking regenerates the AST. Same inputs, radically different outputs. ③ Structurally isomorphic to the Buddhist notion of "破执" (cutting attachment) — the attachment isn't to the conclusion, it's to treating an assumption as a fact. ④ Common misuse: trying to re-derive every small daily decision is cognitive inflation. First principles is a scarce, high-leverage tool reserved for path choices, technical direction, root goals. Everyday decisions should run on cached heuristics. Knowing which decisions deserve first-principles treatment is itself the meta-skill. ⑤ The output is often not a breakthrough plan — it's seeing which layer you were locked into. That alone produces non-linear returns.

Practice: ① List 5 "facts" you hold about a problem. For each ask: "Is this a law of physics, or did someone do this once and it stuck?" ② Pick an industry consensus, assume it's wrong, push three steps downstream — often a viable non-consensus path appears. ③ Don't force a new conclusion every time; arriving back at the same layer counts as success — you verified that layer is a real axiom, not inherited.

Classic Example

2002, Musk costed batteries for Tesla. Decomposed to raw materials (carbon, nickel, aluminum, steel, polymers), London Metal Exchange spot was ~$80/kWh. Finished market price: $600/kWh. The missing $520 is manufacturing, branding, supply chain margin — reorganizable, not physical ceiling. Not a physics breakthrough — a demotion of "market price" from axiom to variable. The entire Gigafactory logic unfolds from that one demotion.

Scenario · BigCat

AI super-individuals get most stuck on "toolstack = someone else's" — whichever model the community pushes, whichever IDE config, whichever prompt framework. First principles view: what are the actually incompressible constraints in your workflow? Token cost? Latency? Context window? Your own attention budget? Mark those as axioms and treat everything else as reshufflable. Most "AI workflows" are community templates pasted onto your problem — IR-layer peephole. First principles is deriving the needed architecture from your specific objective function. Same in investing — "the market says this stock is worth X" is a quote, not a truth. In distributed systems — "CAP three-pick-two" was an engineering tradeoff at one point in time, not a law of physics; modern systems slide along that axis depending on partition state. Tagging every "that's just how X is" as a suspect assumption is the daily first-principles operation.

Reflection

What "industry consensus" in your work have you never personally verified? If you assumed it's wrong — what would three steps downstream look like? Why has nobody done it? Because it's truly infeasible, or because everyone shares the same inherited assumption?

Second-Order Thinking

Others react too; the system is not static
Deep Dive

Howard Marks: "The first-level thinker says, 'It's a good company; let's buy the stock.' The second-level thinker says, 'It's a good company, but everyone thinks it's a good company, so the price is too high; sell.'" The difference isn't information — it's whether you've modeled "everyone else is reacting too" into your decision. First-order thinking treats the environment as a constant — I do X, the result is X's direct consequence. Second-order thinking recognizes reflexivity: you're not analyzing a fixed landscape, you're also reshaping it.

Non-trivial insights: ① First-order vs second-order isn't "shallow vs deep thinking" — it's different system models. First-order: environment is const. Second-order: environment is a function of everyone's actions. ② Programmer's view, most direct: a local optimization drops P99 latency 50% → first-order: "I improved UX." Second-order: "QPS triples, origin gets swamped, DB falls over" — local win, system failure. Every "local optimum, global disaster" story is missing a second-order step. ③ Same root as Nash equilibrium — Nash equilibrium is the stable point after everyone does second-order reasoning. First-order thinkers get priced out before the equilibrium settles. ④ Edge thinking — competitive advantage doesn't come from "I saw it"; it comes from "I saw it, others saw it too, but they stopped at first order." This is what Marks means by "second-level thinking is what produces investment success". ⑤ Over-second-ordering is the opposite trap — "I think N levels, my opponent thinks N+1, so they'll reverse, so I should reverse..." — infinite recursion that doesn't converge in practice. Good decisions usually stop at second or third order, just not first. Going deeper is usually overfitting.

Practice: ① Before finalizing a decision, ask: "And then what? And then what?" — most judgment errors expose themselves at the second "and then." ② Distinguish "if only I do this" from "if everyone with the same incentive does this" — only the latter is the equilibrium. ③ In any highly-replicable environment (AI, investing, careers), always compute the equilibrium, never the initial-state payoff.

Environment is not a const; it's a function of everyone's actions
Classic Example

Rent control. Government sees rents are high → first-order: "Cap them, rent gets cheaper." → Implementation → second-order: landlords exit the market, maintenance drops, black market emerges, new supply stops → long-run housing availability is worse. This script has replayed in 1970s NYC, 1990s Stockholm, 2020s Toronto. Not because the intent was wrong — because the first-order model was right and the second-order model was missing. Nearly every "obviously well-intentioned policy that made things worse" case is a missing second-order step.

Scenario · BigCat

"AI makes everyone 10x more productive." First-order: "I'll ship more." Second-order: "all peers ship 10x too, the field gets denser, excess returns compress to zero, what's left is average return + greater anxiety." Real alpha in the AI era isn't "I use AI" — it's "I used AI to do something others wouldn't do even with the same AI". That's the scarce thing after second-order pricing. Same for parenting: every parent pushes harder → admission bars rise → your kid does 10x the work, relative position unchanged → society-wide Red Queen treadmill. First-order sees the local slope of "input → output"; second-order sees the equilibrium where everyone applies the same input. Distributed systems: cache hit rate improves, RT halves — first-order win; upstream sees better RT, doubles QPS → cache fills up, hit rate drops → avalanche. Systems thinking ≈ second-order thinking in engineering form.

Reflection

The last decision you made — how many seconds did you spend on each of "and then what? and then what?" If you assume your competitors are doing the same action — how much of the first-order expected payoff remains? Where in your past did you win locally but lose the system?

Inversion

"Invert, always invert."
Deep Dive

Jacobi's maxim: "Man muss immer umkehren" — "one must always invert." Charlie Munger engineered it into daily practice: "List the ways something can completely fail, then avoid them." Why is this more useful than "how to succeed"? The failure mode space is smaller and structurally more stable than the success path space. "Doing everything right is hard; avoiding all fatal errors is learnable."

Non-trivial insights: ① Survivorship bias makes forward attribution unreliable — the "successful people did X" sample doesn't include people who did the same X and failed. But the "failures all did Y" sample is cleaner — they're silent, but the data remains. Inversion is the engineering route around survivorship bias. ② Failure space is low-dimensional — most catastrophes reduce to a few archetypes: cash flow died, trust collapsed, technical debt avalanched, key relationship snapped. Success space is high-dimensional — every successful company in the same industry has a non-repeating path. Low-dimensional is learnable; high-dimensional isn't. ③ Structurally isomorphic to test-driven development — good engineers write edge-case tests before the implementation; good security engineers ask "what can break this?" before "what makes this work?" Same cognitive pattern. ④ Same structure as the Buddhist Four Noble Truths — not first describing "what enlightenment is" but first recognizing suffering, then its cause, its cessation, and the path. Structural inversion: start from what you want to avoid, not what you want to reach. ⑤ Inversion's limit — pure defense generates no alpha. It gives you a necessary-but-not-sufficient condition: avoiding fatal errors is the prerequisite for staying in the game, but staying in the game ≠ winning. Use inversion as the foundation, build forward thinking on top — not as the whole.

Practice: ① For any important decision, first list: "if I wanted this to fail spectacularly, what would I do?" — usually 10 items in 5 minutes. Cross-check against your current plan. ② Don't ask "how to be healthy long-term"; ask "how to wreck my body fast" — answer is immediately clear (sit all day, sleep too little, sugar overload, isolation). Direct inversion is high signal density. ③ For interpersonal/family/investment decisions, inversion priority ≥ forward planning. The cost of failure in these domains far exceeds the marginal upside of success.

Classic Example

Munger's USC Law commencement speech: "If I want to know where I'm going to die, I'll never go there." A concrete version: how to make life miserable? — be unreliable, envy, take revenge, drink heavily, never learn, never correct mistakes. Avoid those six and "miserable life" probability drops sharply — while forward "how to be happy" lists can rarely produce executable items at the same quality. That's inversion's leverage: a low-dimensional negative space produces high-quality constraints.

Scenario · BigCat

Parenting: don't ask "how to raise an excellent child" — high-dimensional, answers degenerate to platitudes. Ask "how to make a child completely hate learning" — answer is razor sharp: constant negation, tying outcomes to identity, comparison with others, removing autonomy, turning home into a test prep zone. Once listed, most parents discover they hit 3+ daily. That's inversion's alpha in parenting: derive daily behavior from the failure modes you want to avoid. Same for AI workflow — don't ask "what's perfect AI collaboration"; ask "what makes my AI workflow useless": ① treating AI as a search engine, not a collaborator; ② copying without verifying; ③ outsourcing my core thinking instead of auxiliary computation; ④ no feedback loop. Investing: don't ask "how to get excess returns" — high-dimensional, not learnable; ask "how to guarantee blowing up within 10 years" — leverage, concentration, borrowed money, short-term mindset. Avoid those four and average return already beats 90% of retail investors.

Reflection

Pick a goal you care about right now. Spend 5 minutes listing 10 paths to "completely wreck this." How many is your current behavior on track for? Avoiding those — is the ROI higher than "doing more right things"? Why haven't you run this exercise yet?

Occam's Razor

"Entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity" — not "pick the simple one"
Deep Dive

14th-century William of Ockham: "Pluralitas non est ponenda sine necessitate" — entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity. Commonly misread as "pick the simplest explanation" — a dangerous simplification. The precise form is: when explanatory power is equal, prefer the one introducing fewer unnecessary variables. The premise is equivalent explanatory power. If the complex theory explains things the simple one can't, the razor doesn't apply — quantum mechanics is far more complex than classical mechanics, and more correct.

Non-trivial insights: ① The razor shaves not "entities" but "ad hoc explanations" — patches stuck onto an existing theory to preserve it. Geocentrism added epicycles to explain planetary retrograde motion, then more for each new observation — that ad hoc accumulation is the razor's actual target. ② "X doesn't like me" vs "X is running a complex interpersonal strategy targeting me" — the first is simpler, but simplicity isn't why it's right. It's right because it explains all observed data without introducing unobserved variables. Hanlon's Razor ("never attribute to malice what stupidity explains") is this principle applied to attribution bias. ③ Structurally isomorphic to Karl Friston's predictive processing — the brain itself runs Occam's Razor: it uses the simplest prior compatible with sensory input. That's why first intuitions are often right — and also why biases are hard to dislodge by counterexample: same mechanism. The razor is a cognitive default, not a neutral tool. ④ Three typical misuses: (a) using the razor to suppress a complex-but-correct explanation ("there must be a conspiracy" inverted — cutting away complex possibilities); (b) shaving without verifying equal explanatory power; (c) equating "simple" with "short" — simple means fewer variables + cleaner causal chain, not fewer words. ⑤ Einstein's correction: "Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler." The second half is the operative clause. Shave too far and you lose explanatory power.

Practice: ① For each explanation, two checks: does it really introduce fewer variables than the alternative? Is explanatory power actually equivalent? Both yes → shave. ② Before using "coincidence" or "luck" as an explanation, ask: is it because I haven't found a more structural cause? Luck is real, but it's the most-abused razor terminus. ③ When you see someone else's complex theory, apply the razor first; when you see your own simple conclusion, apply the inverse razor first — alternate feet when walking.

Classic Example

Copernicus's heliocentrism vs Ptolemy's geocentrism. By the 16th century the Ptolemaic system had accumulated 80+ epicycles to match observations — it "worked," but every new observation required another patch. The Copernican model initially didn't predict more accurately (still assumed circular orbits) but radically reduced variable count. That's the razor's template: not "the new theory is more simply correct" — but "with equivalent explanatory power, the new theory has fewer variables." Kepler's elliptical orbits later let heliocentrism pull ahead on accuracy — but the razor had already selected which model the scientific community would invest in extending.

Scenario · BigCat

Prompt engineering razor: 90% of "complex prompt templates" (chain-of-thought + roleplay + multi-clause constraints) — replace with "one clear instruction + one good example" and quality holds or improves. LLMs are sensitive to noisy demonstrations; every constraint you add can suppress the signal you actually want. That's Occam directly applied to prompt engineering: unless a constraint provably changes output quality, shave it. Investing: when a stock underperforms, the simple explanation ("core business is declining") vs the elaborate one ("I'm reading the strategic positioning of a sophisticated investor") — shave the latter unless evidence demands it. Parenting: kid is melting down today — simple ("she slept badly / is hungry") vs elaborate ("she's expressing deep protest at our parenting philosophy") — usually the simple one holds equal or better explanatory power. Wrapping behavior in elaborate psychological narratives is the modern domestic epicycle. Distributed systems: 99% of "mysterious bugs" have boring causes (config, clock skew, network partition); shave all romantic "this is a deep distributed consistency bug" explanations and check boring causes first.

Reflection

The last explanation you wove for something — did it really use fewer variables than the alternative? Or did you wrap a simple cause in layers of dramatic narrative? Conversely: to defend an old judgment, how many epicycles have you quietly added this past week?
