Signal-to-Noise Ratio

Your attention bandwidth is finite — don't let noise drown the signal.
Deep Dive

The signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) comes from information theory and electrical engineering: in any channel, useful information (signal) always travels alongside irrelevant or interfering content (noise). When noise exceeds signal, the receiver cannot extract anything of value. Ported from the physical layer into the cognitive layer, the concept reveals a brutal reality: the modern problem is not information scarcity but noise overload. The information explosion is, fundamentally, a noise explosion; the amount of true signal has not grown proportionally.

Non-trivial insight: most people try to solve information anxiety by "consuming more," which is exactly the wrong direction — adding input while noise dominates only lowers SNR further. The real strategy is "reduce noise," not "amplify signal." From an information-theory standpoint, the value of filtering equals or exceeds the value of producing: a great curator is rarer than a great creator. A deeper insight is the camouflage of noise — the most dangerous noise is not obvious junk but content that looks like signal: trending headlines, plausible analyses, opinions that "sound reasonable." The test is one question: "did this information change my action?" If it changed no decision or behavior, it is noise, no matter how interesting.

How to apply it: build a tiered information architecture — tier one is "action signal" (information that directly drives decisions); tier two is "background signal" (deep knowledge that updates mental models); tier three is noise (everything else). Audit your sources regularly with the question "what would I miss if I stopped this feed for three months?" Switch from push to pull — don't passively accept feeds; actively search with a specific question.

Classic Example

Nassim Taleb argues in Antifragile that the more often you check the news, the higher the share of noise you absorb. Every hour: about 95% noise. Once a day: about 80%. Once a month: SNR improves dramatically. Truly major events will reach you anyway — they punch through every filter — while the "breaking news" of any given hour is overwhelmingly worthless within a week. A newspaper editor's core work is not producing information; it is filtering finite signal out of infinite noise.

Scenario · BigCat

As an aspiring AI super-individual, you face a daily flood of AI news — new models, new frameworks, new papers, new tools, new takes. Without an SNR filter, your attention gets eaten by noise like "LLM benchmark rankings shifted" and "another AI wrapper product." Strategy: tier your AI sources. Tier 1 (signal): technical changes that directly affect your current project (an API change in something you use). Tier 2 (background): 2-3 curated deep-technical reads per week, used to update mental models. Tier 3 (noise): unsubscribe from every "AI daily digest" and replace with a monthly retrospective. Test: after reading, has your workflow changed in any way?

Feynman Technique

If you can't explain it simply, you don't really understand it.
Deep Dive

The Feynman Technique is named after physicist Richard Feynman and rests on a single premise: true understanding means being able to explain something in plain language to someone with no background knowledge. The procedure: pick a concept → pretend to explain it to a child → notice where the explanation breaks down → return to the source material to clear it up → re-explain in simple language → repeat until it flows.

Non-trivial insight: the real power of the Feynman technique is not "simplifying" — it is "exposing the gaps in understanding." Most people think they understand a concept because they can recognize the relevant terms and frameworks. That is "recognition understanding," not "generation understanding." Recognition is passive — you see the answer and think "yes, that's right." Generation is active — you can reconstruct the concept from a blank page. The Feynman technique forces you out of recognition into generation, surfacing every hidden "I thought I understood but actually don't." A deeper point: simple is not a synonym for shallow. Compressing complexity into a clean explanation requires seeing through surface complexity to the underlying structure — itself the highest form of thinking. Einstein's "if you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough" makes the same point.

How to apply it: for every new concept, write a "5-year-old version" — no jargon, only analogies and everyday language. Wherever you get stuck is a black hole in your understanding. Even better: teach someone. Feynman himself deepened his own understanding through lecture preparation — teaching is the best form of learning. The AI-era variant: feed your concept to an AI and have it role-play a student who keeps asking "why?" — see if you can field every follow-up.

Classic Example

Teaching physics at Caltech, Feynman insisted on the most intuitive explanations of quantum electrodynamics. His Feynman diagrams turned wildly abstract particle interactions into visual symbols you could reason about intuitively. That was not "dumbing down" — it was finding an expression closer to the essence than mathematical formulas. His Lectures on Physics remain among the best physics textbooks ever written, because every page does the same thing: reach the deepest essence with the simplest language.

Scenario · BigCat

While learning AI agent architecture, if you cannot explain "what is an AI agent" to your school-age child without using any technical term, you may not really understand it. Try this: "Imagine you have a super-smart helper. You say 'plan a family trip for me,' and instead of giving you an answer right away, it thinks for itself — 'what do I need to know? Budget, destination, length, who's going' — and then it goes off to check weather, compare prices, read reviews, and helps you decide step by step. That's an AI agent — it doesn't just answer questions, it figures out the next step on its own." If you can explain it like that, you have grasped the essence (autonomous planning + tool use + iterative reasoning). Wherever an explanation breaks down, that is your understanding gap.

Knowledge Graph

Isolated knowledge is dead — connected knowledge is alive.
Deep Dive

In its original technical sense, a knowledge graph is a graph structure that represents knowledge with nodes (entities) and edges (relationships) — Google's knowledge graph, Wikipedia's semantic network, and the protein interaction network in biology are all textbook applications. As a mental model, the core insight goes further: the value of knowledge does not live in the nodes themselves (single facts), but in the connections between them (relationships and structure). Someone with 100 isolated facts is far weaker than someone with 50 facts who knows how they link together.

Non-trivial insight: the knowledge-graph mindset exposes the essential difference between "erudition" and "wisdom." Erudition is owning many nodes; wisdom is owning rich edges and high-dimensional connections. This explains why cross-disciplinary thinkers tend to produce breakthrough insights — they have built unexpected edges between fields, and those cross-domain edges are where innovation concentrates. From network science, knowledge graphs exhibit the "small-world phenomenon" — a few long-range connections collapse the distance between seemingly unrelated knowledge domains. Deliberately practicing such long-range links (for example, "what structural isomorphism exists between Buddhist emptiness and the measurement problem in quantum mechanics?" or "what does the consistency problem in distributed systems share with the alignment problem in team management?") systematically increases the connectivity and retrieval efficiency of your knowledge graph. Another key: hub nodes (highly connected core concepts) in the graph have outsized explanatory power — mastering a handful of hubs covers a huge problem space.

How to apply it: for every new concept, force yourself to answer three questions — (1) which three concepts I already know does it most resemble? (2) which seemingly unrelated field shares its underlying structure? (3) which of my existing beliefs does it refute or refine? Use those three classes of edges to keep growing your knowledge network. Physical tools: use mind maps, Obsidian's backlinks, or Notion databases to externalize your knowledge graph and make the connections visible.

Classic Example

Darwin's breakthrough on evolution was, in fact, a "long-range connection" in a knowledge graph. He linked Malthus's population economics (limited resources causing competition) with field observations of species (variation + heredity) to produce the core concept of natural selection. Those two nodes belonged to economics and biology respectively — before Darwin, nobody had drawn an edge between them. Similarly, Shannon connected Boolean algebra with circuit design and built the foundation of digital circuits; Steve Jobs connected a calligraphy class with computer fonts and rewrote the aesthetic standard of personal computing. Every paradigm-level innovation is, at heart, a never-before-drawn edge in the knowledge graph.

Scenario · BigCat

Your cross-disciplinary interests — AI, Buddhism, quantum mechanics, neuroscience, complexity science — are themselves an enormously valuable knowledge graph. The key is to deliberately build the cross-domain edges. For example: the Yogācāra "seed–manifestation" model ↔ "synaptic weight–activation pattern" in neuroscience ↔ "parameters–inference output" in AI — all three describe the same structure: how a stored latent pattern becomes manifest behavior when triggered. The moment you draw that edge, all three understandings deepen at once. Practical tip: maintain a "cross-domain connection log" in your notes. Each week, force yourself to write three entries of the form "X in domain A and Y in domain B are structurally isomorphic because…" That is the systematic process of turning erudition into wisdom.

Forgetting Curve

Memory is not a storage problem — it is a retrieval problem.
Deep Dive

The forgetting curve was proposed by the German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus in 1885: without review, newly learned information decays exponentially — about 42% forgotten within 20 minutes, about 66% within a day, about 75% within a week, and almost everything within a month. The curve's shape and slope are affected by the material's meaningfulness, the learner's emotional state, and the depth of encoding, but its basic exponential decay is extremely robust.

Non-trivial insight: the most overlooked insight about the forgetting curve is not "we forget fast" — it is that forgetting is itself a feature, not a bug. Computationally, forgetting is the brain's garbage collection — if nothing were forgotten, retrieval efficiency would collapse (imagine a never-cleaned filesystem). Forgetting maintains SNR: low-frequency information is automatically downweighted; high-frequency information is reinforced and retained. That leads to the key strategy — Spaced Repetition: do not repair after forgetting; review at the critical point just before forgetting happens. Each review at the critical point flattens the curve — 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 30 days, the interval roughly doubles each time. A deeper cognitive-science point: memory consolidation depends not on "storage strength" (the information is stored) but on "retrieval strength" (the information is retrievable). The Testing Effect shows that active recall is 50-100% more effective for consolidation than passive re-reading. "Read the notes three times" is much worse than "close the book and recall three times."

How to apply it: use a spaced-repetition system (Anki, SuperMemo) for important knowledge and outsource memory to algorithms. More importantly, change how you study: after each session, close the material and actively recall the core points — the "feeling of difficulty" in that moment is the consolidation happening. Connect new knowledge to your existing knowledge graph (depth of encoding) — that is more effective than mere repetition. In the AI era, distinguish between "must memorize" (core mental models, key principles) and "can outsource to AI" (details, data, specific steps), and concentrate spaced repetition on the former.

Classic Example

Polish programmer Piotr Wozniak built the SuperMemo algorithm on top of forgetting-curve research in the 1980s, founding the spaced-repetition field. Using his own system, he learned multiple languages and a huge volume of subject knowledge, and over decades validated the staggering efficiency of spaced repetition: with a fixed 15-20 minutes of daily review, you can maintain long-term memory of tens of thousands of cards (knowledge points). The core principle is triggering review at the exact moment forgetting is about to occur — too early wastes effort, too late requires relearning; the critical point is the efficiency optimum.

Scenario · BigCat

When you study mental models themselves, the forgetting curve issues a sober warning: the four models you read today, with zero review, will be a vague impression a week later. Counter-strategy: (1) immediately close the page and retell each model's core insight in your own words — active retrieval, 100% more effective than re-reading; (2) recall a second time tonight, a third tomorrow, a fourth next week — doubling intervals; (3) even smarter, teach it — in everyday talk with your child, use a life scenario to explain SNR or the Feynman technique: "look, we have so many toys here. If we pick the five favorites, isn't choosing easier than facing a hundred? That's signal-to-noise ratio." When you can teach it, the forgetting curve has lost most of its grip — teaching triggers active retrieval, deep encoding, and emotional connection at the same time.

English Summary

Ebbinghaus's Forgetting Curve shows that newly learned information decays exponentially — about 66% lost within 24 hours without review. But the deeper insight is that forgetting is a feature, not a bug: it serves as the brain's garbage collection, maintaining information retrieval efficiency by deprioritizing low-use data. The optimal counter-strategy is Spaced Repetition — reviewing at the precise moment forgetting is about to occur. Each review at the critical point flattens the curve, doubling the interval before the next review is needed. Crucially, active retrieval (trying to recall with the book closed) is 50-100% more effective than passive review (re-reading). The "Testing Effect" confirms that the difficulty of recall is the mechanism of consolidation. In the AI era, distinguish between knowledge worth committing to memory (core mental models, key principles) and knowledge to outsource to AI (details, data, specific procedures), focusing spaced repetition resources on the former.
