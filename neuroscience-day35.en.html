Mental Models Daily: Neuroscience & Brain Foundations

Neuroplasticity

Hebbian Learning — "Neurons that fire together, wire together"
In Depth

In 1949 Hebb proposed: "Neurons that fire together, wire together." The brain isn't factory-locked hardware — it's a plastic system that rewires daily based on usage patterns. Synaptic strength tracks the statistical correlation of co-firing; cortical maps are remodeled by frequency of use. A London cab driver's posterior hippocampus is measurably larger than average; an amputee's hand-cortex gets "annexed" by neighbors. This isn't metaphor — it's MRI-measurable structural change.

Non-trivial insights: (1) Plasticity is bidirectional. "Use it or lose it" is literal. Unused connections are actively pruned, not passively forgotten. So "I'm not using X, so I'm staying neutral" is wrong — you are actively losing X. (2) Every "doing" is training. Procrastinating once, scrolling shorts once, yelling at your kid once — these aren't isolated events; they reinforce the corresponding circuit's weight. Bad habits aren't "moral weakness" — they're the result of repeated training. (3) Deep learning analogy: neurons = parameters, synaptic strength = weights, lived experience = training data, attention = learning rate. Your daily behavior = SGD loop on yourself. The only difference: a model doesn't know it's being trained — you can. That's the only leverage metacognition provides. (4) Plasticity heavily depends on "attention + emotional intensity" — not all repetition counts. BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) only releases significantly under focus and moderate stress. 100 distracted reps far underperform 10 fully attentive ones. This is the neural basis of deliberate practice. (5) Adult brains remain highly plastic (Merzenich overturned the 20th-century "fixed after maturity" dogma), but pruning is slower than in children — meaning the same change takes longer and demands higher intensity, not that it's impossible.

Practice: (1) Rephrase "I am an X kind of person" as "I'm training the X pattern daily" — identity is a snapshot of process, not its start. (2) Building a new habit: dial up "attention + intensity" first (isolated environment, rested state, clear intent). Low-intensity high-frequency is weaker than high-intensity low-frequency. (3) Killing an old habit: pure abstinence is slow (pruning rate is low). Actively use a new behavior to occupy the same trigger context — deny the old circuit its firing opportunity; only then does pruning accelerate.

Synaptic weight = cumulative function of usage; there's no "idle" state
Classic example

Maguire's 2000 MRI study of London cab drivers — who must pass "The Knowledge," memorizing 25,000 streets — found: (1) drivers' posterior hippocampus (spatial memory) was significantly larger than controls; (2) the difference scaled with years of service; (3) it shrank post-retirement. A clean dose-response. One of the cleanest human demonstrations of adult plasticity: cortical maps reshape under usage, not as metaphor.

BigCat scenario

For an AI/ML practitioner, the engineering reading is direct: (1) Your daily workflow is fine-tuning your own model. Eight hours of interruption + short-video gaps trains the "shallow task-switching + instant-dopamine response" circuit; 90 minutes of continuous deep work trains the "long-context holding + delayed gratification" circuit. You're training both — the only question is which gets more data. (2) The plasticity tax of "AI doing it for me." Long-term outsourcing of code, email, and summaries to AI puts your "thinking-structure" circuit in pruning state. Short-term productivity gains, long-term loss of core capability plasticity. The real risk of the AI-super-individual path isn't "being replaced by AI" — it's pruning away the circuits you most needed to keep while using AI. The countermeasure: explicitly distinguish "AI accelerating what I already know" (augmentation) from "AI replacing what I should be training" (erosion). (3) Parenting transfer. Your child's daily attention distribution is the circuit she's training. Short-video diet = sub-second switching circuit; sitting with her to solve one real problem = long-context holding circuit. "Quality time" is neurally measurable — full BDNF release vs shallow switching.

Reflection

List three things you do most often this week — which circuits are you actively training? Now list three things you "used to be good at" but haven't done in a long time — how far has pruning gone there? Which circuit do you assume is still intact but is actually degrading continuously?

Predictive Processing

Free Energy Principle — The brain doesn't receive the world; it guesses the world
In Depth

20th-century neuroscience assumed the brain was a bottom-up perception machine — input → feature extraction → cognition. In the 21st century, Karl Friston and colleagues overturned this: the brain is fundamentally a prediction machine. It continuously generates predictions about the next moment's sensory input and only propagates the prediction error upward. 95% of your conscious experience is prediction; 5% is error correction. You "see" a world your brain has largely constructed, then verified.

Non-trivial insights: (1) "Reality" is a constrained hallucination. Visual blind spot, change blindness, seeing words that aren't there — these aren't bugs; they're predictive-processing byproducts. You think your eyes sample data; actually the brain guesses, then uses minimal saccades to spot-check. (2) Emotion = interoceptive prediction error (Lisa Feldman Barrett). Anxiety isn't "a reaction to the future" — it's the error the brain generates while predicting your body's next state. This is why reframing works: you're not changing the facts; you're changing the priors the brain predicts from. (3) Bayesian analogy + active inference: brain = continuously running Bayesian model; prior = past experience; likelihood = current sensation; posterior = the "reality" you experience. When priors are too strong, evidence cannot penetrate — the neural basis of confirmation bias. Too weak and you fall into chaos (one model of schizophrenia). (4) The counterintuitive corollary of "active inference": you don't only update predictions to match the world — you also change the world to match predictions. Eye movements, head turns, actions — all minimize prediction error. This is the "free energy minimization" principle. (5) Engineering implication: 80% of your "judgments" are priors running, not evidence computing. The first step to better judgment isn't more evidence — it's calibrating priors by deliberately exposing yourself to counterexamples.

Practice: (1) When emotion spikes, ask "what was the brain predicting? what's the error?" — split the feeling into prediction + error, instead of treating it as fact. (2) To see clearly, manufacture error signals on purpose — deep-talk with someone holding the opposite view; read a book arguing against your position. Broken predictions are the only chance for updates. (3) When you "already know" the answer, you're likely running on priors. Find three pieces of counter-evidence before deciding.

Downward: predictions. Upward: errors. Conscious experience is the stable solution of this loop
Classic example

The McGurk effect (1976): a video shows lips mouthing "ga" while the audio says "ba" — you hear "da." The sound never changed; the brain fused visual prior and auditory evidence into a "compromise answer" and delivered it to consciousness as fact. Crucially, the effect persists even when you know the trick. Lesson: you think you're "hearing," but the brain is guessing. In this sense the whole of conscious experience is a McGurk effect — most of the time the guess just happens to match reality closely enough that you don't notice.

BigCat scenario

For someone with a distributed-systems/AI background, predictive processing is essentially "the brain = a live world-model," with several high-leverage implications: (1) Most meeting/comms misunderstandings are prior collisions — two people hear different things in the same sentence not from missing info, but because each prior decoded the same signal into a different posterior. Asking the other to paraphrase your meaning is engineered error-surfacing. (2) The investing prior trap. The moment you "already understand" a sector, your prior locks in; new evidence gets folded into your preset. Munger's "I'm not entitled to an opinion unless I can argue the other side better than my opponent" is a forced prior-refresh meta-rule. (3) Parenting. Your read of your child (she's lazy / seeking attention / pushing back) is mostly prior running, not observation. The scarcest parenting capacity is "suspend prior, just observe for 30 seconds." (4) AI collaboration. An LLM is also a prediction machine; your prompt calibrates its priors. Good prompt engineers are essentially prior engineers. Knowing your brain runs on the same mechanism makes you more accurate about failure modes of both — "hallucination" in humans is called "taking it for granted." (5) Buddhist interface. The neural basis of "observing" practice is down-weighting prior-driven generation and up-weighting current error signals. "Seeing things as they are" = reducing prior dominance. This translation lets Eastern contemplation and modern neuroscience shake hands.

Reflection

Your strongest emotional reaction in the past 24 hours — what was the brain predicting? what was the error? If you didn't hold that prior, how would the same event be decoded? Which prior have you been running for years without exposure to a single counterexample?

Default Mode Network (DMN)

What the brain does "when not focused" determines who you are
In Depth

In 2001 Marcus Raichle's fMRI work uncovered a counterintuitive fact: when you're not doing a task, a specific brain network gets more active — the Default Mode Network (DMN; medial prefrontal, posterior cingulate, precuneus, etc.). It processes: self-reference, recalling the past, imagining the future, mentalizing others, mind-wandering, daydreaming. The brain's energy use during "nothing" is comparable to focused work. The DMN isn't a bug — it's the brain's "narrative generator," continuously weaving the "who am I" story.

Non-trivial insights: (1) DMN and the task-positive network (TPN) are anticorrelated — one rises as the other falls. Deep work = suppressing DMN; mindfulness = suppressing DMN; rumination = DMN takes over. Your day is essentially time allocation between these two networks. (2) The double-edge of DMN. It's the source of creativity, empathy, and self-reflection — and the breeding ground of anxious rumination, self-criticism, and depressive loops. Same network, different programs, completely different outputs. A neural feature of depression is over-active DMN over-coupled with affective regions — the brain stuck in a "negative story about self." (3) "Boredom" is the gateway to creativity, not its enemy. DMN is most active without external task; remote association, cross-domain integration, insight all depend on this state. Filling every gap with shorts = permanently interrupting DMN = chronic creativity starvation. This is the attention economy's most hidden cognitive theft. (4) Engineering analogy: DMN is the background GC + index rebuild running during idle. Looks like "no output" short-term; determines system health long-term. Never letting GC run = fragmentation accumulates = eventual crash. (5) Buddhist interface. Mindfulness imaging studies consistently show stable practitioners have lower DMN resting activity and reorganized connectivity between DMN and executive control. The "self" is plausibly a DMN narrative product; "no-self" states correspond to transient DMN quieting. Not metaphor — fMRI-measurable.

Practice: (1) Reserve windows for DMN — 15-30 minutes daily of "no input": walk without earbuds, shower without phone, do simple chores without podcasts. This is hardware-level dependency for creativity and metacognition. (2) Distinguish two DMN modes — open exploration (remote association, insight) vs anxious rumination (self-critical loop). Preserve the former, interrupt the latter (any external focus task ends rumination). (3) Beware the "efficient-filling" trap — packing every minute with "useful" input looks optimal but chronically erodes insight generation.

DMN / TPN are anticorrelated: your day is fundamentally a budget allocation between the two
Classic example

Killingsworth & Gilbert (2010) randomly sampled 5,000+ people via phone: "What are you doing? Thinking about what you're doing? How happy are you?" Result: 47% of waking hours people were mind-wandering (DMN state). The key finding wasn't the high wandering rate — it was that people are significantly less happy when wandering, regardless of whether wandering content was positive or negative. Conclusion: "Where your mind is matters more than where your body is." This quantifies DMN's rumination mode. But Beaty et al.'s work shows high-creativity individuals have more flexible coupling between DMN and executive networks — they can run DMN in "open exploration" mode rather than rumination. The difference isn't how active DMN is — it's which program it runs.

BigCat scenario

For the AI-super-individual path, DMN is a severely underrated "creativity infrastructure": (1) Insight doesn't come from work hours. Most complex technical solutions surface during walks, showers, half-sleep — only DMN does remote association (cross-problem-domain linking). Always-focus mode = always-local-search = never escaping the current basin. (2) "Efficient filling" is chronic creativity suicide. Podcast in commute, scroll in queue, video while cooking — each segment looks "efficient," but the aggregate zeroes out DMN windows. The truly scarce resource for super-individuals isn't info input — it's digestion + recombination idle time. (3) Distinguish DMN rumination vs DMN exploration. Both feel like "thinking." Rumination is the closed loop "I screwed up again / why did she say that / what if next month"; exploration is "if I connect X to Y... / that client's problem rhymes with that project five years ago..." Interrupt the former (move, do something); protect the latter (keep walking). (4) Parenting transfer. Your child's "boredom" is her DMN developmental window. Every time you hand her a screen or a class because she's bored, you're depriving the "self-narrative generator" of training. A child who can sit with 30 minutes of boredom and one who is permanently stimulated have structurally different creative capacities as adults. (5) Consciousness interface. Twenty minutes of mindfulness sitting is essentially a DMN reset — observing which program it runs, not getting absorbed, letting it settle. Long-term practitioners show weakened "self-referential story" tagging on fMRI — the neural equivalent of the Buddhist "no-self."

Reflection

How many "no-input" windows did you give yourself in the past 7 days (no phone, no audio, pure wandering)? What state was your most recent meaningful insight born in — focused work or DMN? Is your DMN mostly running "exploration" or "rumination"?

Dopamine Reward Prediction Error

You think dopamine is "pleasure" — it's actually "surprise"
In Depth

In the 1990s Wolfram Schultz's monkey experiments rewrote the dopamine story: dopamine neurons don't fire when reward arrives — they fire when reward exceeds expectation. Expected reward → silent; surprise reward → large spike; expected but withheld → significantly below baseline. This is the reward prediction error (RPE). Dopamine signal = actual reward − expected reward. If that formula looks familiar to RL engineers — yes, the TD-learning δ signal is essentially the same thing. Schultz's findings directly inspired modern RL's neural foundation theory.

Non-trivial insights: (1) Dopamine is not the "pleasure molecule" — it's the "wanting molecule." It drives pursuit, not satisfaction. Berridge distinguished "wanting" from "liking" — dopamine only does the former. This explains a counterintuitive phenomenon: pursue X obsessively → get it → feel nothing → pursue next. Not your fault — designed that way. (2) "Surprise" is the core currency. Predictable rewards don't trigger dopamine, so everything "just starting" hits hardest: first three months of a new job, first six months of a relationship, the moment a new project is kicked off. Once stable and predictable, signal decays → you think "passion fading," but it's RPE → 0. Not a defect — designed to encourage exploration of new states. (3) Variable-ratio reinforcement is the most efficient addiction design. Gambling, scrolling Twitter, shorts — terrifying because rewards are unpredictable; every swipe might be the "surprise." RPE never zeros out. This is the cleanest case of the brain being reverse-engineered. Understanding this is when you see clearly: it's not "weak willpower" — you're playing against an opponent custom-built for your brain. (4) RPE reverse-use: deliberately engineer low-expectation scenarios so normal outcomes become positive RPE. Lowering expectations isn't pessimism — it's smart dopamine management. The same coffee tastes wildly different after an hour of food-video scrolling vs first thing in the morning. Expectations were pushed up. (5) AI analogy: human dopamine = brain-level TD-learning; reward signal = internally generated scalar. Understanding this reveals: you're not driven by the outside — you're driven by your own internally generated prediction error. And predictions can be recalibrated.

Practice: (1) Set hard isolation for addictive inputs. Shorts, social, news feeds are variable-reward machines. "Scrolling less" doesn't work (the system is engineered for you to fail). Environment isolation (device/account/time-window) is more reliable than willpower. (2) Manage expectations to protect dopamine. Important work shouldn't be driven by "stimulation peaks"; keep many rewards in the unpredicted zone (don't pre-imagine the success scene in detail — that spends your RPE budget). (3) Accept "low-RPE but meaningful" daily — deliberate practice, long-term intimate relationships, raising a child. Low dopamine signal (highly predictable), highest cumulative value. Understanding this lets you keep going through the dopamine silence.

Dopamine encodes "reward − expectation," not reward itself
Classic example

Schultz's monkey experiment: (1) Light → juice. Early: dopamine fires at juice. (2) After repeated learning: dopamine fires at light, not juice (predicted). (3) Light without juice: at the expected juice-time, dopamine activity drops below baseline. The data say plainly: dopamine doesn't encode "how much you got," it encodes "how much it differed from expectation." Schultz's δ = r − V(s) is almost identically shaped to Sutton & Barto's TD-error — one of the cleanest cases of bidirectional cross-pollination between computational neuroscience and machine learning.

BigCat scenario

For someone with an RL background, RPE is the most engineering-friendly model in neuroscience: (1) Reverse-engineering shorts/social. Their core algorithmic objective is "keep your RPE from zeroing" — making rewards persistently unpredictable. You can't stop scrolling because you don't know if the next clip is better. Knowing this, "I'll scroll less today" is engineering-infeasible — you're fighting an RL system tuned to your brain. The only reliable countermeasure is cut the environment (device/account/time window), not willpower competition. (2) Explaining "post-achievement emptiness." Funding closed, promotion landed, child got into the school — the prediction has been simulated many times; achievement-time RPE is near 0; no joy of the imagined magnitude. Not depression — design as intended. Knowing this prevents the "emptiness invalidates the entire effort" misread. (3) Parenting. "Surprise rewards" beat "promised rewards" by a lot — same toy, promised in a week vs unexpectedly brought home today: the latter generates much bigger RPE. But the deeper insight: do not train your child into an RPE chaser. Daily screen rewards = training her dopamine system to be desensitized to "low-RPE real life." The most hidden intergenerational consequence of the attention economy. (4) Engineering yourself. Important work first (when dopamine is fresh, expectations not yet flattened); less Twitter before writing (protect the "starting to write" RPE); leave surprise windows in weekly plans (don't predict every moment full). RPE isn't abstract — it's a daily-schedulable resource.
