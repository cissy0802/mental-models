Mental Models Daily: Neuroscience & the Neural Substrate

2026-05-27 · Day 35

Neuroplasticity

"The brain can change" — yes, but it isn't free. Every reshaping squeezes something else.
Explanation

Neuroplasticity is the structural rewriting of neural circuits under experience — synaptic strength, dendritic branching, myelination, and modest neurogenesis. It's the substrate of "the brain can change" — and the most overused neuro-slogan. "The brain can fine-tune like an LLM" is correct; the unspoken second half is that fine-tuning has cost, has windows, and has rules — not "want it, grow it." Hebb's full rule: "fire together, wire together; out of sync, lose the link." Strengthening one circuit prunes another. Cortical surface, energy, synaptic proteins are zero-sum.

Non-obvious points: ① Plasticity ≠ free plasticity. Adult writing requires three co-occurring inputs: attention/salience (ACh + NE open the plasticity gate), prediction error (VTA dopamine flags "something to learn here"), and sleep consolidation (NREM replay + REM integration commit the day's weights). Miss one — experience leaves no trace. That's why reading 100 papers can yield no growth; why long but distracted practice barely teaches. ② Pruning and strengthening are equivalent — circuits you repeatedly don't use are flagged "not important" by the same system and quietly dismantled. Pianists' finger cortex shrinks if they stop; same for any unused circuit. "Use it or lose it" is physical fact, not moral slogan. ③ Critical periods are temporary plasticity peaks — infant visual/language circuits stay "unlocked" briefly, later sealed by perineuronal nets. Sealing is necessary — otherwise each new experience would overwrite stable structure, the LLM catastrophic-forgetting problem. ④ Adult windows aren't gone, just default-locked — they can be pried open by strong emotion + intensity + repeated exposure + adequate sleep. Meditation, new language acquisition, instrument practice, drastic environment change have all been shown to briefly elevate adult cortical plasticity. ⑤ Negative plasticity is equally real — chronic stress shrinks hippocampus and PFC; chronic short-video reshapes attention networks. Every day you are shaping or being shaped; there's no neutral middle. ⑥ AI-collaboration risk: outsourcing "thinking something through" to AI = actively starving that circuit's plasticity input. The less you write, the faster the writing circuit atrophies — not a metaphor, a physical fact. The dark side of "AI super-individual" is using AI to thin one's own brain. ⑦ Buddhist link: vāsanā (habit-energy) describes STDP accumulating at the metabolic level — every passing thought etches a groove; seeing this is the entry point of practice.

Practice pivot: before deciding to "learn something new," ask whether the three co-occurring conditions can land together — can you bring full attention? Can you manufacture prediction error (not passive scrolling)? Can you protect sleep? Missing more than one — don't start; you'll only self-soothe, the brain won't write. Then flip the question: which loop am I repeating but wishing gone — did I feed it one less time today?

Adult plasticity is an AND-gate — three co-occurring inputs, no substitutes
Classic Example

Maguire et al. (2000) scanned London taxi drivers who must memorize "The Knowledge" (25,000 streets + tens of thousands of landmarks). Senior drivers' right posterior hippocampus was significantly enlarged, with size proportional to years driving, but their anterior hippocampus shrank — cortical real estate is zero-sum; the expanding map-memory squeezed other spatial functions. The first hard evidence of structural plasticity in adult humans, and a vivid demonstration that "plasticity is budget allocation, not free expansion." Retired drivers who stop driving show slow re-shrinkage — "use it or lose it" is a material fact, not a metaphor.

BigCat Scenario

① You unlock your phone 80 times a day = you are precisely training a "distraction circuit" 80 times daily, with all three conditions met: high red-dot salience, unpredictable content (strong prediction error), last reinforcement right before sleep. One hour of deep work can't compete — not from weak will, but a 1:80 dosage as objective neural fact. ② Correct posture for learning a new framework: not a weekend 8-hour binge, but 3 weeks of daily 25 minutes with a specific problem in hand + good sleep that night. With all three conditions met the brain writes; weekend binges without prediction error + sleep are muscle-memory illusion that evaporates in days. ③ Use AI to manufacture plasticity in reverse: don't let AI "quickly explain a concept" — that flattens the necessary prediction error. Instead, prompt it to refute you, supply counterexamples, expose blind spots, guide you to derive it yourself. AI as a high-density prediction-error generator is the real compute leverage for BigCat; AI as explainer thins the brain. ④ Reverse self-check: the things you've done repeatedly this week and wished gone (an unconscious app refresh, lashing out at someone, late-night snacking) — did you feed any of them one less time today? One less feeding = actively dismantling that circuit, more precise than another resolution. ⑤ Parallel for your child: what she does repeatedly is what she's growing — independent of what you verbally encourage. The sum of "what's repeated at home" = the material substrate of her future personality. The lowest-cost, highest-leverage parental intervention point.

AI Prompts
English Prompt
I want to genuinely master [skill / domain / habit], but I keep [forgetting / stalling / failing to start]. Diagnose with the three co-occurring conditions of adult plasticity: 1. Which of attention / prediction error / sleep is most offline? Cite concrete evidence. 2. Design a 3-week cadence: daily dose, format, source of prediction error, sleep protection window. 3. Identify a circuit I'm reinforcing in the wrong direction — high-frequency training of something I want gone — and give one "feed it less once" action this week. 4. Am I using AI as an explainer (flattening prediction error) or as a challenger (manufacturing it)? Suggest a concrete shift.

Predictive Processing

You don't "see" the world — you predict it, and let the senses correct the prediction. "Reality" is a sensory-constrained hallucination.
Explanation

Classical model: senses → brain processing → perception. Predictive Processing flips it: the brain is a constantly running prediction engine; the senses only transmit "how wrong the prediction was" (prediction error) upward. What you see ≈ the brain's best guess, sensory-corrected — Andy Clark's "controlled hallucination." Friston's Free Energy Principle extends this to the brain's single objective: minimize prediction error (≈ minimize surprise) — perception, action, learning, emotion are all special cases.

Non-obvious points: ① Perception updates predictions to match data; action updates data to match predictions; learning updates priors — three solutions to one objective. This is why merely watching someone pour water activates motor cortex similarly to actually pouring — prediction is already driving motor preparation. ② Emotions are interoceptive predictions. Anxiety = high-uncertainty prediction about internal state; depression = locked-in "no matter what I do, reward is low" prior; panic = interoceptive prediction error exploding. This is why "just relax" almost never works, but high-quality interoceptive data (deep breathing, cold water, exercise, mindfulness) does — it updates the prediction. ③ The "certainty" of perception is prior-amplified — under strong priors, ambiguous data gets force-interpreted into expected form. A senior's "I can see it immediately" is high-quality priors, but the same priors entering a new regime (LLMs/agents) become reverse baggage — your "should be" suppresses "actually is." ④ Hallucinations, illusions, empathy, self-fulfilling prophecies are different doses of the same mechanism: priors too strong → hallucination; priors distorted → illusion; priors aligned with another → empathy; priors shaping action that reshapes data → self-fulfilling prophecy. ⑤ Fully isomorphic to Bayesian thinking — prior + likelihood → posterior. The brain is an approximate variational-Bayesian inference engine, always running. ⑥ Same primitive as LLMs — next-token prediction and cortical predictive coding are silicon vs carbon implementations of the same computation. Transformer success isn't coincidence; it approximates the brain's core operator. ⑦ Yogācāra Buddhism mapped this 1600 years ago — "phenomena are mind-constructed" describes top-down perception's core: you don't see the world, you build it. ⑧ Isomorphic to the quantum observer effect — the observing process inseparably shapes the observed; priors inseparably shape the "objective" you see. ⑨ AI-collaboration risk: an LLM that always agrees flattens your prediction-error signal — your world model stops updating. A people-pleasing AI is a cognitive sedative; long-term use ossifies your model. Healthy collaboration prompts AI to actively refute.

Practice pivot: write one line each day for "what surprised me today". Surprise is the only place learning happens — zero surprise = either your model has frozen (cognitive ossification) or you've stopped exposing yourself to new data. Reverse self-check: when you've recently caught yourself feeling "I knew it," was that the prior compressing inconvenient data? Find yourself one counterexample.

Hierarchical predictive coding · top-down predictions (blue) vs bottom-up errors (red)
Classic Example

Adelson's "checker-shadow illusion": squares A and B have pixel-identical luminance, but your visual system, holding the high-level prior "shadowed squares should be brighter than they look," automatically up-corrects B. You're not seeing pixels — you're seeing your brain's corrected best guess. Cover the surrounding context with paper exposing only A and B — the illusion collapses; the context that produced the prior is gone. This is top-down prediction overriding bottom-up data, naked-eye demonstration — and it's the neural reason "I saw it with my own eyes" claims need humility. The same mechanism makes eyewitness testimony, interview scoring, and taste rating systematically biased by any prior cue — you never see pure data, always data × prior.

BigCat Scenario

① The cost of seniority: 15 years of coding, one glance at a new framework, "oh, it's just X" — strong priors compressing sensory input. The details a novice notices are auto-filled by your prediction engine. Antidote: deliberately enter domains where your templates don't apply (Buddhist shoshin / beginner's mind trains exactly this); or instruct AI in your prompt to "assume I know nothing here, restate at the sensory level." ② Debugging = locating maximum prediction error. Senior engineering intuition is a precision prior about where things fail. But the same prior dragged into a new regime (LLMs/agents) becomes reverse baggage — you reach for familiar failure modes and miss the real issues in the new ecosystem. ③ Neural definition of anxiety: high-uncertainty interoceptive prediction. Deep breathing/mindfulness works because it supplies high-quality interoceptive data to update the prediction, not vague "relaxation." This is why "think positive" fails for real anxiety — you're feeding higher-layer propositions without updating the underlying interoceptive prediction. ④ Family scene: your "she's just like that" about your child is a high-strength prior that filters out evidence she's changing. Before any judgment, ask: "Am I looking at her right now, or auto-completing my prediction?" ⑤ AI use: an AI that always agrees = your world model stops updating. Make "refute me, find the weakest link in what I just argued" part of your default prompts. ⑥ Quantum link: observer participates in shaping the observed — not just physics, but how cognition itself works. "Objectivity" is the product of prior × data; pure objectivity doesn't exist.

AI Prompts
English Prompt
My current beliefs about [topic / domain / decision / a person]: [list priors / predictions]. Run a predictive-processing diagnostic: 1. Which of these are strong vs weak priors? Where might priors be overwriting inconvenient data? 2. Generate 3 high-quality prediction-error inputs — counterexamples, counter-questions, counter-data. Challenge me directly. 3. For each item: is the fix to update the prior, or to change action so the data changes? 4. If emotion is involved, name the likely interoceptive prediction underneath it. 5. Am I using you (the AI) in a way that flattens my prediction error? Suggest a prompt shift.

Default Mode Network (DMN)

The network most active when you're "doing nothing." It builds the thing you call "me" — and it's the easiest thing for modern life to starve.
Explanation

The DMN is the network most active when you're "doing nothing" (mPFC + PCC + angular gyrus + hippocampus), burning energy comparable to the task-positive network (TPN). "Rest" isn't idle; it's switching to another high-energy process — self-referential thought, autobiographical memory, mental time travel (recall + future imagination), theory of mind, mind-wandering, moral judgment, value integration. Raichle et al. (2001) overturned the old "idle brain shuts off" model — at "rest" the brain uses less than 10% less energy than during focused work.

Non-obvious points: ① The DMN constructs "the self" — it stitches "past me + present me + future me" into a continuous narrative. Carhart-Harris's fMRI showed psilocybin reduces DMN internal connectivity, with subjective ego dissolution as the correlate — "I," as a felt presence, depends on a continuously running process. Long-term meditators show analogous DMN modulation — "non-self" at the neural level is reduced DMN dominance. ② Double-edged: creative recombination and insight pre-computation happen here (the shower "aha" is the DMN cross-shuffling the day's inputs); but so does rumination — a core neural feature of depression is over-active, sticky DMN. ③ DMN and TPN are anti-correlated — one up → the other suppressed. Healthy brains switch freely; unhealthy ones get stuck on one side. Always task-positive = creative drought + blurry self; always DMN-dominant = rumination + depression. ④ Neural diagnosis of modern affliction: phones keep you task-positive (every notification is a switch back), chronic DMN starvation → reduced creativity, weaker consolidation, blurred value sense, decision fatigue. The "boredom crisis" is DMN malnutrition; modern people's "fewer original ideas" is not talent decay but DMN deprivation. ⑤ Engineering analog: DMN ≈ background GC + index rebuild + offline learning + retrospective logs. A cluster that never runs background work will OOM or have slow-queries fan out. Adding cron jobs for background work is engineering common sense; scheduling DMN time is the same common sense. ⑥ Buddhist link: "act without dwelling" — the ideal isn't DMN-off (that's autobiography loss), it's DMN tunable, enter freely, exit freely. ⑦ AI-collaboration risk: filling every idle gap with AI (a query while compiling, a write while building, walking-companion chat) = zeroing what little DMN time remains. The most dangerous anti-pattern for a high-utilization user like BigCat — on the surface you're "exploiting fragments," in fact you're killing the only windows where insight pre-computation can happen. Many find "more AI = fewer new ideas" — that's the neuroscience.

Practice pivot: schedule 30+ minutes daily of "audio-free DMN time" — shower without audio, walk without podcast, commute staring out the window. Rule: phone in pocket ≠ DMN online — you're still expecting it to buzz; task-positive remains on standby. Then check for rumination: if DMN themes loop on "I should / how could they / if only..." → you've entered depressive rumination; you need a theme-switcher, not more DMN time.

DMN ⇌ TPN seesaw · always-on TPN = chronic DMN starvation
Classic Example

Carhart-Harris et al. (2012, 2016) administered IV psilocybin under fMRI: DMN internal connectivity dropped sharply, with subjective correlate "ego dissolution / space-time deconstruction." Follow-up showed long-term meditators (> 10,000 hours) display analogous resting-state DMN modularity reductions. Among the hardest evidence that the "self" is a brain process, not a substance — you don't have a self; the self is a continuously running process. Switch it off and experience remains, but the feeling of "I" disappears. This maps directly onto Madhyamaka/Yogācāra analyses of selfhood — millennium-old phenomenology made visible by 21st-century fMRI. Same reason long-distance runners, deep meditators, artists in flow, and lovers at peak intimacy all report "the self vanished" — the DMN momentarily ceded ground.

BigCat Scenario

① Your best architectural insights arrive in the shower or on a walk — not coincidence. TPN collected the problems during the day; DMN recombines in the background. Shower and walks are the cheapest "switch-to-DMN" entries. Bring the phone into the shower = kill the GC process. ② AI-era trap: a query while compiling, a write while building, "let me discuss with AI" on a walk — feels efficient, in fact zeros what little DMN time remained. The most dangerous anti-pattern for a high-utilization user like BigCat: the more "efficient" you become, the more starved the DMN, the lower creativity, the more you depend on AI for ideas — negative loop. ③ Rumination warning: if the DMN keeps replaying "why didn't I... / if they thought I..." → you've entered depressive rumination. Mindfulness isn't stopping the DMN; it's installing a theme-switcher — once noticed, redirect to a wider aperture ("does this matter in 5 years?" / "what can I influence now?"). ④ For your child: let her be bored. Don't fill every 5-minute gap with a tablet. "Boredom" isn't a child's failure — it's the growth hormone of the DMN; a zoning-out child is self-building. Packed enrichment schedules = stripping her future ability to "think up new things." ⑤ Team management: meetings forever + Slack forever = team-wide TPN lock; collective creativity dries up. Make "agenda-less 1:1 walks" a default. ⑥ Buddhist link: "without dwelling" at the neural level is tunable DMN — not turning it off (that's autobiography loss) but not being trapped by it. Practice = installing a throttle on the DMN.

AI Prompts
English Prompt
My cognitive / emotional state this past week: [describe]. Run a DMN / TPN scan: 1. How many deliberate DMN sessions did I have (audio-free shower / walk / commute / staring)? Quantify. 2. Am I drifting into rumination — recurring theme and typical time of day? Do I need more DMN time, or a DMN theme-switcher? 3. Am I filling every idle slot with AI / phone, starving the DMN? Name the 3 most dangerous fillers. 4. Prescribe the minimum this week: one DMN protection + one rumination-interrupt + one TPN load-shed. 5. Which AI habit of mine is most stealing DMN time? Suggest a swap.

Dopamine Reward Prediction Error

Dopamine is not the reward chemical — it's "reality minus expectation." Addiction, stalled learning, lost momentum: same equation.
Explanation

The most common misconception about dopamine is "the reward chemical" / "the pleasure molecule" — wrong. Schultz's seminal VTA recordings in monkeys proved dopamine encodes reward prediction error, δ = R − V, not reward itself. Expected rewards barely fire; surprise rewards fire +; predicted-but-omitted rewards fire −. This equation is mathematically identical to TD-learning in reinforcement learning — AlphaGo's TD update and your VTA neurons execute the same equation, carbon vs silicon implementation aside.

Non-obvious points: ① Expected reward fires no dopamine — month 2 of a promotion already feels flat, year-5 romantic dinner becomes routine. Dopamine is numb to "level" and sharp to "delta." Steady-state happiness isn't sustained by holding a high-water mark; it's sustained by continuous mild positive δ. ② The peak lives on the cue, not the reward. Anticipation before unlocking the app is stronger than the content itself. Berridge's wanting ≠ liking — two independent circuits. Addiction is the wanting system decoupled from liking (you want more, like less). ③ Variable-ratio reward is the hardest to escape — δ never converges to zero. Slots, social feeds, short-form video, email red dots all run the same algorithm. Skinner knew it; the TikTok team knows it; your VTA neurons are programmed for it. Not a willpower problem, a math problem. ④ RPE is the engine of learning — no prediction error = no learning. Perpetual "good job" zeroes the learning signal; perpetual criticism keeps V below R, suppressing dopamine into learned helplessness (one neural face of depression). Effective feedback = slightly above prediction + not fully predictable. Why the "always-5-star mother" and the "always-finding-fault boss" reach the same endpoint — both flatten δ. ⑤ Closes the loop with plasticity — VTA's δ signal is literally what gates dopamine-dependent plasticity. So "practice without surprise teaches no skill" has a molecular name: no dopamine-gated plasticity. ⑥ Buddhist tanha: the Buddha separated wanting from satisfaction 2500 years before Berridge. "Suffering's structural root isn't lack — it's that the wanting system never quenches: once one prediction is satisfied, the next rises, δ chases anew. Seeing this isn't destroying the wanting system (impossible, unnecessary), it's not being driven by it — the neural definition of non-attachment. ⑦ AI-collaboration risk: AI interactions are extremely fast-looped, reward is highly variable (each reply differs), cue is highly salient (one button to invoke) — this is the perfect form of variable-ratio addiction. The more you use AI, the more the "let me ask" impulse itself gets reinforced into a near-addictive loop. Healthy use: make AI rewards less unpredictable (designated time slots, batched questions), move the dopamine peak from cue ("let me try asking") to outcome ("what I solved with it").

Practice pivot: examine anything you can't stop or have lost momentum on — sketch its δ curve. Can't stop = variable high δ; lost momentum = δ → 0. The first's antidote is cutting the cue or lowering the variance; the second's is manufacturing visible prediction error (new difficulty ramps, new collaborators, surprise constraints). Effective feedback for child / team / self = specific + slightly above expectation + occasionally surprising — all three or you aren't feeding the learning engine.

RPE = R − V · expected = no learning; variable-ratio keeps δ ≠ 0 = permanent hook
Classic Example

Schultz et al. (1993, 1997) trained monkeys: bell → juice. Day 1: VTA fires when juice arrives ("unexpected good"). Post-training: the firing peak migrates onto the bell — the cue now carries the prediction; the reward itself is expected. Critical test: after training is complete, withhold the juice — at the predicted delivery time the VTA fires below baseline. Naked-eye R − V: V is established but R never arrives, δ is negative. This firing pattern was later identified as the δ term of TD-learning — computational neuroscience and reinforcement learning unified by one equation. Not just an animal experiment; the mathematical language of learning itself, found pre-written in mammalian midbrain.

BigCat Scenario

① TikTok/X/short-video addiction's neural definition: variable-ratio reward + high-frequency cue. The next clip might be hilarious or boring — δ never converges; VTA runs forever. Not weak will, but a precision attack on a vulnerability of your reward-learning algorithm. The fix isn't "watch less" (willpower vs algorithm always loses); it's cut the cue (uninstall, kill all push notifications, remove from home screen) or lower the variance (RSS / subscription replacing feed). ② Real principle of feedback to a child: perpetual "good job" equals withholding the juice post-training — the V of praise is already predicted, δ → 0, the learning signal vanishes. She looks "praised" but neurally gains nothing. Effective feedback = specific + slightly above expectation + occasionally surprising ("I noticed that step was different — how'd you think of it?" beats 100 "good job"s). ③ Engineering homolog: if your reward function (OKR/KPI) is fully predictable, it stops driving learning in any agent — including teams and yourself. Inject unpredictable, high-signal feedback. This is what Karpathy means by "surprise is data." ④ Self-management: the thrill of starting something new decays fast — the project didn't get worse, V caught up with R, δ → 0. The fix is manufacturing visible prediction errors: difficulty ramps, surprise constraints ("this week only one new tool"), new collaborators bringing new data. ⑤ Most dangerous AI trap: AI interactions are naturally variable-reward (every reply differs), extremely salient-cued (one button), fast-looped — the perfect addictive form. The "let me try asking" impulse is being reinforced into a near-addictive loop. Healthy use: designate time slots, batch questions, move the dopamine peak from cue ("let me ask") to outcome ("what I solved with it"). ⑥ Buddhist mapping: tanha never quenching is RPE's structural feature. "Non-attachment" isn't destroying the wanting system (against physiology) — it's seeing wanting ≠ liking and not being driven by the cue. A daily moment of "right now, am I wanting or liking?" is the contemporary version of meditation. ⑦ Cross-model link: RPE's δ is the dopamine gate that triggers neuroplasticity; δ is also the central quantity in predictive processing; DMN dominates when δ ≈ 0 and yields to TPN when δ ≠ 0. Today's four models are one engine viewed from four cross-sections, not four separate things.
